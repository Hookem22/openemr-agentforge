## Sulfonamide antibiotics and hypersensitivity

A documented allergy to sulfonamide antibiotics (e.g., sulfamethoxazole, commonly given combined
with trimethoprim) is a true contraindication to re-exposure absent a formal desensitization
protocol or confirmed negative allergy testing. A prior reaction described as hives, urticaria, or
angioedema indicates a genuine IgE-mediated or similar hypersensitivity risk, and re-exposure risks
a reaction of equal or greater severity, including anaphylaxis.

## Cross-reactivity with non-antibiotic sulfonamides

Cross-reactivity between sulfonamide antibiotics and non-antibiotic sulfonamide-containing drugs
(certain diuretics, sulfonylureas) is now understood to be low and is not a reliable basis alone
for withholding an unrelated non-antibiotic sulfonamide medication; however, cross-reactivity
between different sulfonamide antibiotics themselves is expected and clinically significant.

## Medication reconciliation and allergy checking

Any newly prescribed or newly reported medication should be checked against the patient's
documented allergy list before administration or continuation, regardless of the indication for
which it was prescribed (e.g., a UTI) or how recently the allergy was recorded; an allergy entry
does not expire and remains clinically active unless explicitly reversed by the recording
clinician after formal evaluation.

## Recommended action on discovering a conflict

When a patient is found to be taking a medication that conflicts with a documented allergy, the
finding should be flagged explicitly for clinician review rather than assumed to be an intentional,
already-evaluated exception -- silent omission of the conflict is not appropriate.
